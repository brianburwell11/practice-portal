The [mobile audio playback research](/docs/mobile-audio-playback/) argues that audio format matters more than you'd expect for a practice tool — not because of fidelity at normal speed, but because time-stretching amplifies codec artifacts. MP3's pre-echo swoosh, invisible at 1x, becomes a metallic shimmer at 0.5x. This page lets you hear it for yourself.

## Try It

Pick a sample (drums, horn, vocals), choose a format, and slow it down. Switch between formats at the same point in the track to A/B compare. The differences are most obvious on transients — drum hits, note attacks, consonants in vocals.

## What You're Hearing

When you slow audio down, you're stretching small moments of sound into longer ones. Any compression artifacts in the source get stretched too — like zooming into a JPEG and seeing the block boundaries.

**FLAC** is lossless. There are no artifacts to reveal, so it sounds clean at any speed. The tradeoff is file size: roughly 4-5x larger than Opus for the same audio.

**Opus** at 128 kbps is the best lossy codec for this use case. Its design produces fewer of the "smearing" artifacts that become audible when stretched. At moderate slow-down (0.7x-0.85x), most listeners can't distinguish it from FLAC.

**MP3** at 192 kbps sounds fine at normal speed, but its reliance on the modified discrete cosine transform produces characteristic pre-echo artifacts around transients. At 0.5x, drum hits develop a metallic "swooshing" quality that's absent in the other two formats.

## Technical Notes

This demo uses a plain `HTMLAudioElement` with the browser's native `playbackRate` control — the pitch shifts proportionally with speed (lower pitch at slower speeds). Practice Portal's actual playback engine uses SoundTouch for pitch-corrected time-stretching, which preserves the original pitch but amplifies codec artifacts even more than raw speed change. If anything, this demo understates the difference between formats.
